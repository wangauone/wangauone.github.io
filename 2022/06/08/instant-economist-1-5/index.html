Chapter 1 - How Economist Think

What is economic?

  • To answer basic questions: What - What is produced / How - How it is produced / Who - For whom it is produced
  • Spectrum: Government control → Individual freedom - No silver bullet
  • Economics is not about predicting the future or taking political sides. It is a framework for thinking about the questions.

How economist think?

  • Trade-offs should be taken seriously.
    • Choices can help some people and hurt others.
    • Economists care about all the statistical people who area hurt or helped, not just individual faces in a news report.
      • “The plural of anecdote is not data.”
  • Self-interest can be an effective way of organizing a society.
    • Self-interest when directed appropriately, can provide broad social benefits.
      • Want less → discourage with a tax; Want more → encourage with a subsidy
        • Use less oil → Tax it
        • Develop more fuel-efficient cars → Subsidize the technology
        • Use more solar power in the home → Give tax credits
  • All costs are opportunity costs.
    • The true cost is not the money you spent; it is the thing you give up.
  • Prices are determined by the market, not by a producer.
    • Landlords raise rents not because they want to — they always want to — but because market conditions of supply and demand shift in a way that allows them to do so.

Chapter 2 - Division of Labor

  • Simple consumer goods are produced through a complex process
    • A pencil is cheap but what it takes to make a pencil is expensive.
  • Division of labor
    • Specialization → Productivity
      • No need to learn to produce everything you need.
      • The richer the nation, the more apparent that the inhabitants needs aids to survive.
    • “Economies of scale”: the larger, the cheaper.
      • $100 factory produces 100 products → $1 per product
      • $100 factory produces 1 product → $100 per product
  • Economy with a high division of labor
    • Warehouse: Put in & Pick up
      • Balance relies on a value system
      • Value (Price for product, Salary for labor) is determined by the supply and demand.

Chapter 3 - Supply and Demand

  • Market - Supply & Demand
  • Goods market
    • “value in exchange” vs “value in use”
      • Diamond water paradox
    • Economist talks about the exchange value, which is based on scarcity.
  • Price - Supply & Demand
  • Demand: The relationship between the price and quantity demanded of a good.
    • Downward slope: Price drops, quantity demanded increases
      • Causes: Substitution effect and income effect
    • Demand is the curve. Quantity demanded is a point.
    • Rise - Move right & Fall - Move left
      • Quantity demanded: Price
      • Demand: Society income rises, Population boom, Tastes & fads, Price of a substitute good, etc.
  • Supply: The relationship between the price and quantity supplied of a good.
    • Upward slope: Price increases, quantity supplied increases
      • Causes: Profit and new firms
    • Supply is the curve, Quantity supplied is a point.
    • Rise - Move right & Fall - Move left
      • Quantity supplied: Price
      • Supply: Change in production technology, weather, Price of inputs, etc.
  • Equilibrium: At the price where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied.
    • A point toward which a market tends but not to say the market is always at equilibrium.
      • Not sure how long and how close

Chapter 4 - Price Floors and Ceilings

  • Price ceiling - A maximum price
    • The forces of supply and demand → Shortage & Ways to get around the ceiling (e.g. reduced maintenance for housing).
  • Price floor - A minimum price
    • The forces of supply and demand → Surplus & Ways to get around the floor (e.g. increased price for farmland).
  • Price regulation draws no distinction between who’s needy and who’s not.
  • Better targeted polices
    • Demand
      • Give money to the poor by raising welfare payments
      • Subsidize buyers of food through school lunch programs
    • Supply
      • Subsidize the construction of low-cost housing
      • Supplement the income of farmers whose farms are below a certain size
  • Political system tends to choose price control
    • Looks like a zero cost since it is not showing on the balance sheet as taxes or subsidies

Chapter 5 - Elasticity

  • Tax (e.g. Cigarette) is aiming at discouraging the use of the product or collecting the government revenue?
    • Elasticity - How price change affects the quantity demanded and supplied.
  • Elasticity of demand: % change in quantity demanded / % change in price.
    • Price rises 10%
      • Quantity demanded falls by 50% → 50% / 10% = 5
      • Quantity demanded falls by 2% → 2% / 10% = 0.2
    • Goods with
      • inelastic demand (<1) → Difficult to substitute → E.g medicine
      • elastic demand (>1) → Easy to substitute → E.g orange juice
      • unitary elasticity of demand (=1)
  • Elasticity of supply: % change in quantity supplied / % change in price.
    • Price rises 10%
      • Quantity supplied rises by 40% → 40% / 10% = 4
      • Quantity supplied rises by 5% → 5% / 10% = 0.5
    • Goods with
      • inelastic supply (<1) → Difficult to expand production → E.g the paintings of Picasso
      • elastic supply (>1) → Easy to expand production → E.g. running below capacity
      • unitary elasticity of supply (=1)
  • Whether demand / supply is elastic, inelastic, or unitary → How prices should be set
    • Rising price → More revenue if demand is inelastic but not if demand is elastic.
      • Idea: When a firm is setting prices, it shouldn’t simply charge as much as possible.
      • Elasticity can be learned from over time by experimenting with slightly higher or lower prices.
    • Demand and supply inelastic in the short run but elastic in the long run.
      • Demand: Gas price up
        • In the short run (less options), pay more.
        • In the long run (more options), move home or electric model.
      • Supply: Expand production
        • In the short run, fairly inelastic.
        • In the long run, quite elastic.
      • Reason for the unstable prices in the short run and stable (not fixed) in the long run.
    • Demand is inelastic → Increases in the cost can be passed to consumers. Demand is elastic → Increase in the cost have to be carried by producers.
      • Energy price up → Higher price (former) or lower output (latter)
      • Cigarette inelastic → tax as the increased cost can be passed to consumers.
    • Elasticity applications: In policy or strategy, a large response to a change in price?
      • Quantity demanded of some goods (e.g. cigarette taxes)
      • Quantity supplied of some goods (e.g. subsidies for clean energy)
      • Quantity supplied of hours worked (e.g. income tax policy)
      • Quantity supplied of amount saved / financial capital (e.g. encouraging personal saving)
